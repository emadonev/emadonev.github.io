Where is the Bang in the Big Bang?

  • Cosmology, Astrophysics
  • 0 Comments

How did the Universe begin?

Where did all the matter and energy come from?

These are the fundamental questions about the Universe. So what are the answers to these questions? Do we even know?

We do. And the theory with which we describe this event is called The Big Bang theory. It's called the Big Bang because it all started with an explosion. But how?
Once, 13.8 billion years ago, a small dot, a millionth of a period at the end of a sentence, contained all the matter (material, everything you see) and energy in the whole Universe! It was very hot and dense ( for example, gold is much denser than water because the same shape made of gold is much heavier than water ). And then it exploded.

Into a dull explosion.Why dull? Because there was no sound from the explosion, and there was no color. Why? Because there was no space for sound to expand into and there was no light, so the explosion was transparent. All in all, it was a pretty dull explosion. But, I will feature it as red, because it is hard to see a transparent explosion.

As soon as it exploded, there was the "Planck era" (this era didn't last very long), named after the German scientist Max Planck, who revolutionized physics and created the quantum mechanics and physics field. Four main forces came to be:

  • Gravity - binding bulk matter
  • Weak force - controlling radioactive decay
  • Strong force - binding atomic nuclei
  • Electromagnetic force - binding molecules

A trillionth of a second after...

There were two particles at that time: quarks and leptons. Quarks are never alone. They always have another quark nearby. And if you want to separate two quarks, they would just stretch until they break, but now each has another pair! (That's because when you pull them apart, you create energy enough for another pair. Or E=mc^2. )

Leptons, though, are loners. They never have a pair. They just push other leptons away. The best-known lepton is an electron.

Furthermore, the cosmos was full of energy contained in little wave packets of light called photons. But these particles routinely kept converting into matter-antimatter particles ( antimatter particles are like evil twins of matter particles ), and then they would collide back into photons. Rarely, about one in a billion didn't find their antimatter particle friend, so they stay just matter, and thanks to these today, we have matter because otherwise, there would be nothing in the Universe.

The Universe was about the size of our solar system. It was below a trillion degrees Kelvin ( A unit of temperature in science, it is 273º more than Celsius. For example, absolute zero in the Universe is -273ºC or 0K)

A millionth of a second after...

The Universe was not hot enough to cook quarks and leptons, so quarks grabbed their dance partners to create more massive particles such as protons and neutrons.

A second after...

The Universe has grown a few light-years across, and the temperature was a billion degrees, Kelvin. The Universe was still hot enough to cook electrons and their antimatter particles' positrons, and they annihilate (destroy) each other and disappear. But again, there was a ratio of one to a billion electrons who survived.

>

The temperature was now below a hundred million degrees Kelvin. Protons and neutrons started to fuse with each other forming atomic nuclei.

Two minutes after...

Protons started to attract electrons to the atomic nuclei when the temperature was below 3000 degrees Kelvin; thus, forming atoms, the building blocks of life! Finally, photons can roam free through the Universe.

380,000 years after...

Gravity started grouping particles forming stars, and grouping them together, creating galaxies.

9 billion years later...

Our Sun formed in a cosmic cloud of particles and dust. This dust started clumping into 8 planets, 100 000 asteroids, and billions of comets. And one of these planets was Earth in the Goldilocks zone, not too hot and not too cold. Perfect for life.

The Universe is older than 9 billion years...

Our planet formed oceans, and molecules started to combine creating life, thus, animals, plants, and eventually us…

What happened before the Big Bang?

We honestly don't know. But maybe someday someone reading this post will find the answer to this deep and mysterious question...
