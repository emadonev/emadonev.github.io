The Universal Laws

  • Physics
  • 0 Comments

In the previous post, we discussed what the universe is made of. In this post, we are going to discuss the universal laws which essentially control the universe.

Of course, there are more laws with which the universe functions, but I am going to mention only the basic ones.

First, we will discuss the most basic and one of the most important laws - gravity. What does gravity do? Essentially it clumps matter together and attracts objects to one another.

Gravity is like the king of the universe. It controls how many stars, galaxies, planets, and other objects will form. It is also greedy for more power. It wants to slow down the expansion of the universe, but dark matter has it covered. It wants to blow up stars when they are born, but the immense pressure is not in gravity's favor. Who discovered gravity? Sir Issac Newton, in the 16th century. He was fleeing from London when the black death came to his home where he grew up. He accidentally discovered gravity due to an apple falling on his head. But, he did do just that. Newton worked with light, colors, calculus, math, telescopes, motion, and gravity. In short, he is a genius.

The next three laws were also Newton's genius. They are the three laws of motion.

1st law of motion: an object will go in the same path forever until another force acts upon it. That means that if you kick a ball, you act a force upon it. To be more precise, your muscle force. The ball flies into the sky, but over a short amount of time, gravity pulls the ball down to Earth, and the ball falls. The force you kicked the ball with also weakens, and that is another factor for the falling ball.

Newton's 2nd law of motion: the higher the mass of an object, the more force it will take to accelerate it. Let us design a thought experiment. Imagine you have an elephant on a skateboard and a mouse on a skateboard. The elephant weighs 1 ton, while the mouse 100 grams. You push the elephant and mouse at the same time, with the same force. You will see that the mouse went much further than the elephant. If you want the elephant to go as far as the mouse, you will need to push it harder. The equation for this law is f = m*a. F is the force, m is mass, and a is the acceleration. That means that the force for which you pushed the elephant depends on its mass and how hard did you push it.

Newton's 3rd law of motion: every action has an equal and opposite reaction. Imagine you stand on a skateboard, and you throw a ball to the front -- you will recoil and move backward with the skateboard.

Finally, the last law is the speed of light. Light is a beautiful thing. It brings you sight and color. But it is also complicated. It has wave-particle duality, and it is the fastest thing in the universe. But it wasn't always this way. Scientists thought that light has a changeable speed. The smartest person in history, Albert Einstein, proved that light has a constant speed (in a vacuum). 300,000 km/s. That is very, very fast. But the universe is huge. We can't really measure it in kilometers, definitely not meters. So what do we do? We measure it with light! There is a measurement of how much does light travel in a year? The answer is 9 460 730 472 580 800 m. We are definitely not measuring the universe in meters.

All of these laws are very important for the universe. As a bonus, I have 3 fun facts about gravity that are weird and fun!

Fun Fact 1: Picture this scenario: you throw a ball towards the ground. It falls down. Nothing more to it, right? Not exactly. When you throw a ball towards the ground, gravity pushes it at 9.807 m/s2. There is an equation - G = m * g. The difference between G and g is that G is the weight of an object, while g is the gravitational constant or 9.807 m/s2. M is the mass of an object in kilograms or pounds.

Fun fact 2: Gravity pulls on you daily, to keep you firm on the ground. But it has health impacts. Gravity pulls hard against the spine if you stand much during the day. From about 6:30 in the morning to about 23:00, you can shrink from about 1-2 cm! That is a lot! So it is vital to exercise often not to shrink!

Fun fact 3: Gravity pulls stuff to Earth, right? But we saw from the fun fact one that it doesn't pull equally on every object. But that is not all. The further away you are from the Earth's core, the less impact gravity has on you. So if you want to find the place with the least gravity on Earth, look for the highest mountains around the equator. Why the equator? Because Earth is not a perfect sphere, so around the equator, it is the farthest place on Earth if you are on 0 m above sea level. So if you want to find the place on Earth with the least gravity, look around the equator, in the highest mountains.
