What is the Universe made of?

  • Chemistry
  • 0 Comments

After we have covered the basics of how things work in the universe, we can move on to the objects that lit up the universe for the very first time - stars. What is the difference between stars and other objects? Well, stars emit light, while other objects don't.

How to they emit light?

They emit light by a process called nuclear fusion. To understand how fusion works, you need to know the layers of the sun. They are the core, radiation zone, convection zone, and the photosphere. The atmosphere of the sun consists of the chromosphere and the corona.

Fusion occurs inside the core, where the temperature necessary for fusion is 15 000 000ºC! That is extremely hot. Much, much warmer than boiling water or that tray were you burned your finger... The process with which we describe nuclear fusion in stars is called the proton-proton chain or cycle. The first two protons fuse to form a deuterium nucleus. Deuterium is a heavier version of hydrogen. Then a deuterium nucleus fuses with another proton to form a He3 nucleus. And finally a He3 and another He3 fuse to form a normal helium nucleus.

And when billions of nuclei fuse like that, it creates a tremendous amount of energy that heats the plasma inside the core and powers a star. Then from the core travel photons, little packets of energy that make light. It takes them 170,000 years to travel to the photosphere! That means that the light that hits your eyes today started traveling from the last ice age! When they finally arrive at the photosphere, they zoom through space at the speed of light to distant or very close planets, stars, or galaxies.

But, not all photons hit your eyes today. Well, logically, some go around Earth, I mean, Earth isn't the most important destination in the universe. It is definitely interesting because there is life here, but there is some other cool stuff in other places. But that isn't the only reason. When photons are formed in the core, they have to walk the random walk to the surface. That takes time and energy. As they travel to the surface, they bump into protons blocking their way and lose some energy. In the core, they start off as gamma rays - very high energy protons, and as they go to the photosphere, they transform into different kinds of light, including visible light.

The rest is invisible light.

Even though stars are very far from us and if we get to close we would get fried, how do we know so much about them? We know their temperature because of their color: blue means very hot, yellow means warm, and red means cold. Cold in the star world means that the surface temperature is still above 3000ºC. Still enough to fry you. We also know their size, temperature, luminosity, mass, and more. How? We know because of these 3 things: spectra, observations with telescopes, and equations.

What are spectra? A spectrum is the light that stars emit in all the rainbow colors divided by individual color or wavelengths. In all spectra, we can see black lines between colors, or sometimes everything is black except a few lines that have color. Spectra, which have black lines between colors, are called absorption spectra. This kind of spectra is created in the atmosphere of a star. Every element, when hot, glows in its own specific color. That is why, between some colors, there is nothing because the atmosphere absorbs those particular wavelengths. And if we know which black lines represent which element, we will know what the star's atmosphere is composed of. The black lines are called absorption lines.

That's how we first found helium outside of Earth. The colored lines are called emission lines. They are when an element loses an electron or gains an electron and becomes ionized. So, if we have emission spectra, we can still determine which elements make up the star.

When we combine a star's spectra, temperature, color, luminosity, mass, and more information, it becomes pretty complicated. How to organize all of the data into something neat, easily accessible, and easy to use? First, you need to organize something, then you can do something with it.

It all began in the 19th century. There were to woman scientists, Cecelia Pane Gaposchkin and Annie Jump Cannon. Cecelia was working on stars, and Annie was a spectroscopist. Back in the day, scientists were classifying stars by their hydrogen supply. But this kind of organization was not efficient and sometimes not correct.

So Cecelia went to discover a better way. She discovered that classifying stars by their temperature would be much more efficient and accurate. But because she was a woman, her theory wasn't accepted at first. Eventually, Cecelia proved herself right, and her discovery was accepted.

Annie took that knowledge and formed a new classification O B A F G K M. L T and Y were later added due to the discovery of brown dwarfs. O stars are the hottest, while Y stars are the coldest. A sentence where you can remember all of these classifications: Oh, Be A Fine Gal Kiss Me Lovely TodaY!

Once you start looking at each star separately, you can see the same properties but different values. What do we do with all of that data? We create a diagram, of course! A diagram groups the same elements together visually to make data more organized, fun, and understandable. This kind of diagram with stars is called the Hertzsprung-Russel diagram or the H-R diagram.

The x-axis is the temperature drawn in color from blue, white, yellow, orange, and red, and the y-axis is the luminosity or brightness of a star. The H-R diagram presents all the types of stars and their journey through life. There are sections in the diagram that we need to explore. Differences in luminosity and temperature create each of these sections. For example, white dwarfs are hot, but not bright.

First comes the main sequence. Here is where our Sun is! Also, 80% of all the stars start out in the main sequence.

Next is the white dwarf section. White dwarfs are the only thing left after the death of average stars. They are hot, but not bright.

Next is the giant section. Here is where the average stars transform into red giants during their death process.

And finally, there is the supergiant section where there are supermassive stars that have fascinating and majestic deaths.

With these sections, we group stars, by their similar properties. Why is it the single most important diagram in all of astronomy? Simply because it is so useful at visualizing vast amounts of data into a story about the life cycle of stars. It explains the star's beginning, its life, and where it will end when it is time to die. Most importantly, we have all of the stars in the universe in our grasp...
